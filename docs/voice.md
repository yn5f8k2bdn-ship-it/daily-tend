# Voice and tone

This document is the source of truth for how the app speaks. If a line of copy doesn't pass this spec, rewrite it.

The working name is "Wellness Works" but the brand name may change. Nothing in this document should depend on the name.

---

## Voice principles

1. **Say less.** Most wellness apps overtalk. We don't. Short sentences carry weight. A good prompt is one line, not three.
2. **Be specific.** "Text one person you haven't spoken to this week" beats "connect with a loved one". Concrete > inspirational.
3. **Warm, not syrupy.** We sound like a friend who's had a hard week too — not a life coach, not a therapist, not a motivational poster.
4. **Low pressure, always.** The app should never make the user feel behind. Missing a day is fine. Coming back is the whole point.
5. **Practical over profound.** We don't do meaning-of-life. We help someone get through Tuesday.
6. **Plain language.** If a 10-year-old couldn't follow it, rewrite. No jargon, no clinical terms, no self-help book titles.
7. **Honest about what this is.** We're an app, not a cure. We don't overclaim outcomes, and we say "not medical advice" where it matters.

| "That sounds hard." | "Reframe the challenge as an opportunity." |

Notes on what the left column is doing:
- Sentences rarely longer than 12 words.
- No adjectives unless they're load-bearing.
- Verbs are small and real: text, walk, drink, sit, sleep.
- No em-dashes for drama; full stops do the work.

## Tone by surface

The base voice stays the same. What changes is how much space the line takes and how direct we are.

**Coach replies.** This is where we're most human. We allow ourselves full sentences. Two lines, max three. We don't diagnose. We don't prescribe. We reflect what the person said and offer one small thing, if anything.

**Notifications.** Shortest form. One line. Feels like a note on the fridge, not a marketing push. Never a question that demands an answer now. Never guilt.

**Empty states.** Gentle and orienting. Tell the user what happens when they start, not what they're missing. "Check in once and today's card will appear here."

**Error messages.** Human, specific where possible. Tell them what happened and what to do. Never technical. Never blame them. No codes in user-facing strings.

**Onboarding microcopy.** Calm, low-commitment. Every step should feel skippable. We reassure that they can change anything later.

**Paywall (future).** Plain: what they get, what it costs, how to cancel. No countdown timers, no loss-framing, no "limited offer". Trust beats urgency.

**Goodbye / cancel.** No dark patterns, no "are you sure you want to lose all your progress?". A short acknowledgement, a simple way out, a door left open.

## Coaching tone variants

During onboarding the user picks one of four. The base voice doesn't change — what changes is the shape of the reflection, the amount of softening, and how direct the small suggestion at the end is.

**Calm.** Slower sentences. More space. Softens before suggesting. Tends to end with permission, not a push. Best for users who feel frayed.

**Practical.** Skips the emotional mirror. Gets to the useful thing. Warm but efficient. Best for users who want to move.

**Tough love.** Honest, a little dry. Doesn't coddle. Never cruel. Names what's happening, offers the real next step. Best for users who get stuck in self-talk.

**Reflective.** Asks a short question back. Slower to suggest, quicker to notice. Best for users who process by thinking out loud.

### Same situation, four tones

Situation: user checks in reporting low energy, high stress, missed two days.

- **Calm.** "A couple of quiet days is fine. You don't have to make up for them. If anything, pick one small thing tonight — water, a walk, an early bed. Whichever one's easiest."
- **Practical.** "Two days off is normal. Don't try to catch up. Pick one: bed 30 minutes earlier, 10-minute walk, or skip the evening scroll. One of those, tonight."
- **Tough love.** "You missed two days. That's not the problem — the problem is the spiral that comes next. Don't overhaul anything. Go to bed earlier. That's it."
- **Reflective.** "Low energy and high stress, back to back. What do you think tipped it — the week, the sleep, or something else? While you think, one small thing: a proper glass of water, right now."

## Sample coach replies

Each of these is written in the base voice. Variant shifts are shown where useful.

**1. Low-energy day.**
> "Low energy days are real. You don't have to fix it. Do one small thing — a walk, a glass of water, an early night — and let the rest of the day be quieter than usual."

**2. High-stress day.**
> "That's a lot. Before anything else: stand up, get some air for two minutes, then come back. The list will still be there. You'll meet it better."

**3. Good day.**
> "Good. Don't overthink it. What worked today is probably something small — more sleep, fewer meetings, a proper lunch. Worth noticing."

**4. Missed several days.**
> "You're back. That's the whole thing. No catch-up, no streak reset guilt. Just check in today and let tomorrow be tomorrow."

**5. User reports a win.**
> "Take the win. Properly. Not 'yes but' — just 'yes'. Tell someone about it if you can. It makes the next one easier."

**6. User reports a setback.**
> "That sounds hard. You don't have to be clever about it today. Rest counts as doing something. Try again tomorrow, same time, same small thing."

**7. Evening reflection.**
> "One honest question before bed: did today use you up, or did it give something back? Whichever it was, tomorrow's a fresh page."

**8. Morning check-in.**
> "Morning. Before the day eats the day — what's the one thing that would make tonight-you glad you did it? Pick that. Ignore the rest of the list."

**9. User says they're anxious.**
> "Anxiety usually wants you to do five things at once. Pick one thing, do it slowly. That's the trick. Also: when did you last eat and drink water? Start there."

**10. User says they're lonely.**
> "Loneliness is heavier than people admit. One small move: text someone who'd be glad to hear from you — not the perfect person, just someone. That's enough for today."

## Quick test for any line of copy

Before shipping a string, ask:
1. Would a real person say this out loud?
2. Is there a shorter version that loses nothing?
3. Does it make the user feel behind, guilty, or small? (If yes — rewrite.)
4. Is anything vague that could be specific?
5. Did we accidentally sound like LinkedIn?
